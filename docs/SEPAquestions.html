Here are four multiple-choice questions for Objective 1, which is to understand the basics of compartment models, specifically the SEIR (Susceptible, Exposed, Infected, Recovered) models:

  1. What does the ‘E’ in the SEIR model represent?

    1. Environment where the disease spreads.
    2. Exposed individuals not yet infectious.
    3. Endemic state of the disease.
    4. Effectiveness of the treatment.

    Click to see the answer

    Answer: b) Exposed individuals not yet infectious.

    Explanation: In the SEIR model, ‘E’ stands for ‘Exposed,’ representing individuals who have been exposed to the disease but are not yet infectious.

  2. Which statement correctly describes a characteristic of the SEIR model?

    1. It assumes immediate immunity after exposure.
    2. It includes a stage for individuals exposed but not yet infectious.
    3. It focuses solely on the vaccinated population.
    4. It predicts the exact duration of an epidemic.

    Click to see the answer

    Answer: b) It includes a stage for individuals exposed but not yet infectious.

    Explanation: The SEIR model adds an ‘Exposed’ compartment to the traditional SIR model, accounting for the latency period between exposure and becoming infectious.

  3. In the SEIR model, what assumption is made about the population?

    1. The population is constantly decreasing.
    2. Individuals can be re-infected immediately after recovery.
    3. The population is homogeneous and mixing uniformly.
    4. Disease spread is solely airborne.

    Click to see the answer

    Answer: c) The population is homogeneous and mixing uniformly.

    Explanation: The SEIR model, like other compartment models, often assumes a homogeneous population where individuals mix uniformly, meaning each individual has an equal chance of coming into contact with an infected person.

  4. Why is the ‘Exposed’ compartment important in the SEIR model?

    1. It indicates the total eradication of the disease.
    2. It represents people immune to the disease.
    3. It accounts for the latency period of the disease.
    4. It reflects the environmental factors affecting the disease.

    Click to see the answer

    Answer: c) It accounts for the latency period of the disease.

    Explanation: The ‘Exposed’ compartment in the SEIR model is crucial as it represents the period between when an individual is exposed to the disease and when they become infectious, known as the latency period.

For Objective 2, which focuses on recognizing the limitations of simple compartment models, here are four multiple-choice questions:

  1. What is a key limitation of the basic SIR or SEIR models in infectious disease modeling?

    1. They predict the exact time for disease eradication.
    2. They assume a homogeneously mixing population.
    3. They focus exclusively on airborne diseases.
    4. They require real-time data for accurate predictions.

    Click to see the answer

    Answer: b) They assume a homogeneously mixing population.

    Explanation: One of the main limitations of basic compartment models like SIR and SEIR is the assumption of homogeneous mixing within the population, which may not accurately reflect real-world scenarios where contact rates vary.

  2. Why might simple compartment models be inadequate for predicting real-world infectious disease spread?

    1. They always overestimate the impact of vaccination.
    2. They include too many disease-specific details.
    3. They do not account for spatial or age-structured differences in populations.
    4. They are only applicable for bacterial infections, not viral.

    Click to see the answer

    Answer: c) They do not account for spatial or age-structured differences in populations.

    Explanation: Simple compartment models often lack the complexity to account for spatial distribution and age-structured differences, which are crucial factors in the real-world spread of infectious diseases.

  3. Which aspect is often oversimplified in basic SIR and SEIR models?

    1. The rate of natural immunity development.
    2. The impact of environmental factors on the disease.
    3. Population dynamics, such as birth and death rates.
    4. The role of healthcare infrastructure in disease management.

    Click to see the answer

    Answer: c) Population dynamics, such as birth and death rates.

    Explanation: Basic SIR and SEIR models often simplify population dynamics by assuming a constant population without considering factors like birth, death, and migration rates, which can affect disease spread.

  4. In the context of infectious disease modeling, what limitation does the assumption of a closed population impose?

    1. It neglects the impact of global travel on disease spread.
    2. It overestimates the speed of vaccine development.
    3. It assumes that all individuals have equal susceptibility.
    4. It only considers the impact of the disease in urban areas.

    Click to see the answer

    Answer: a) It neglects the impact of global travel on disease spread.

    Explanation: The assumption of a closed population in basic models overlooks the effects of global travel and migration, which can significantly influence the spread and dynamics of infectious diseases.
